# What Happens After Your App Launches (And Why Most Agencies Disappear)

Launch day feels like the finish line. It isn't.

The first 30 days after launch are when the real problems show up. Real users behave nothing like test users. Edge cases multiply. That payment flow that worked perfectly in staging? Someone finds a way to break it by Tuesday.

This is exactly when most agencies vanish.

---

## The pattern nobody warns you about

Here's how it usually goes:

1. You hire an agency. They're responsive, excited, full of ideas.
2. They build your product. It looks great in the demo.
3. You launch. Champagne emoji in Slack.
4. Day 3: you find a bug. You message them.
5. Day 5: they respond. "We'll look into it."
6. Day 10: still waiting. Their "senior engineer" is now on another client's project.
7. Day 20: you're patching things yourself or hiring someone new to fix what they built.

Sound familiar? You're not alone. We hear this story from nearly every client who comes to us after working with another agency.

---

## Why agencies disappear

It's not malice. It's economics.

Most agencies operate on hourly billing or milestone-based contracts that end at launch. The moment your project ships, their financial incentive to help you drops to zero. Their best engineers get reassigned to new revenue. Your "maintenance" gets handed to whoever has bandwidth, which usually means a junior or nobody at all.

The contract is technically fulfilled. The invoice is paid. They move on.

But your product is just getting started.

---

## What actually happens in the first 30 days

If you've launched a product before, you know this list:

- **Performance issues under real load.** Staging had 50 test users. Production has 500 real ones hitting the same endpoint simultaneously.
- **User behaviour you didn't predict.** Someone enters their phone number with dashes. Someone uploads a 40MB photo as their avatar. Someone hits "submit" six times in two seconds.
- **Integration failures.** That payment gateway works differently in production mode. The email provider rate-limits you. The third-party API changes its response format without warning.
- **Feature gaps you couldn't see until real people used it.** "Why can't I do X?" shows up in your support inbox within the first week. Every time.
- **Security probes.** Bots find new apps fast. You'll see strange requests in your logs within days.

None of this is failure. This is normal. But it requires someone who knows the codebase to respond quickly.

## The questions to ask your agency before you sign

If you're evaluating development partners right now, ask these before you commit:

1. **"What happens after launch?"** If the answer is vague or requires a separate contract, that's your answer.
2. **"Who handles bugs found in the first month?"** If they say "that depends" or "we can discuss a maintenance package," they're planning to bill you for their mistakes.
3. **"Will the same engineer who built it be available post-launch?"** If they can't commit to this, your post-launch support will be someone reading the codebase for the first time while your users wait.
4. **"What metrics will you track after launch?"** If they don't have an answer, they're measuring success by delivery, not by results.
5. **"Can I see a post-launch support agreement from a previous client?"** Agencies that do this well are proud to show it. Agencies that don't will dodge the question.
